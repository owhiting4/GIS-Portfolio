El Morro Visibility Analysis

Line-of-sight modeling of coastal defense from Castillo San Felipe del Morro, Puerto Rico

Introduction

Old San Juan served as one of Spain’s most important defensive ports in the Caribbean. Castillo San Felipe del Morro, commonly known as El Morro, began construction in 1539 and was built to defend the entrance to San Juan Bay from naval attack. Its location on high coastal cliffs gave defenders a clear, unobstructed view of both the Atlantic Ocean and the harbor entrance.

The fort was part of a much larger defensive system that protected the city for centuries. El Morro endured attacks by the English in 1595 and 1598, the Dutch in 1625, and during the Spanish-American War in 1898. Today, it's preserved as part of San Juan National Historic Site and the UNESCO-listed La Fortaleza and San Juan National Historic Site.

Viewshed Analysis

This map shows cumulative visibility from cannon positions along the walls of El Morro. These points were digitized based on visible terrain depressions in the DEM and field observations made on site, where cannon locations could be directly compared to the surface pattern of the fort.

The viewshed output represents the number of observer points that can see each location. Higher values indicate stronger overlap in visibility, while lower values represent areas seen by fewer cannon positions.

Visibility Range

This supporting map uses buffer distances to show how far visibility extends from the fort. While simpler than the viewshed, it helps orient and gives a better sense of scale for interpreting the broader visibility patterns across the coastline and open water.

Data & Methods

Data

The primary data used for spatial analysis is a DEM from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, found here.

Observer Placement

Observer points were manually placed rather than generated automatically. Their locations were based on field observations and on visible depressions along the fort walls in the DEM that aligned with cannon positions seen during the site visit. This was to reflect actual, meaningful artillery locations instead of generalized point spacing.

Surface Preparation

The raw DEM from the NOAA does not extend very far beyond shorelines, but this analysis is of ocean visibility. To extend the analysis into open water, a polygon was created to fill a 1600 meter buffer around the fort. 1600 meters is the max range of the average cannon at El Morro, according to the National Park Service. This polygon was assigned a constant elevation value taken from the outer edge of the DEM that is over water. The extension polygon was converted to raster and mosaicked to the DEM to create a continuous surface extending into San Juan Bay. The mosaic seams and other elevation values over water were smoothed in order to reduce artificial gaps and holes in the viewshed output that were not meaningful in the context of open ocean visibility.

Analysis Methodology

A geodesic viewshed analysis was then run using the modified DEM and the manually placed observer points. A small observer offset was applied so that the modeled line of sight better reflected cannon placement behind the parapet walls rather than ground-level visibility alone. In addition to the viewshed, distance buffers were created as a second layout to help visualize the outward scale of visibility and make the extent of the analysis easier to interpret.

El Morro: Facts & History

  • El Morro was built to control the entrance to San Juan Bay, making visibility over water one of its most important defensive advantages.
  • The only time El Morro fell to enemy forces-British fleets in 1598- it was because they were able to overwhelm the seaside defense and march on the fort from land where it was much more vulnerable. But only after 65 days of pillaging, the British forces were so deathly plagued by dysentery that they had to give up their position and flee by ship. Some historians claim this failure to hold Puerto Rico pushed England's focus away from the Carribean and towards North America.
  • Losing Puerto Rico even briefly caused Spain to really invest in El Morro, sending more troops and reforming government, trade, and tax policy. This made Puerto Rico one of the most important
  • El Morro was fortified after each time it was attacked over the span of over 200 years. It now stands at 6 levels, with walls 140 feet above the sea and up to 25 feet thick.
  • The last major renovations were done by an Irish engineer, Thomas O'Daly. These renovations were completed just in time for another British invasion in 1797.
  • A century later in 1898, Puerto Rico became an easy target during the Spanish-American War. The defenses built to protect against wooden ships were now no match for the technologies of the Industrial Revolution that America had. This ended the era of Spanish presence in the Americas, and established the United States as a world power.
  • During both World Wars, the United States used El Morro as a military base, installing bunkers, naval observation posts, and anti-aircraft artillery. After hundreds of years defending against wooden sailing ships, El Morro could now defend against air and submarine attacks. It now stands as a fusion of Medieval architecture and modern engineering.
